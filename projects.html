15.7.24 I finished it a few days ago and forgot to update the website on it. The updates also weren't going through, for some reason. Anyway, I'll put up a picture soon. Here's the (work in progress) little paragraph I wrote for it:

The main idea behind this painting is the perception of others and how that is in itself a form of "distortion". Originally, I wanted to explore Sartre's concept that people view themselves as the "subject", or the "doer" and others as "objects" - and that in interacting with others, you are forced to recognise yourself as the "subject" in other people's eyes - something that is existentially difficult for us to grasp.

Though this concept can be applied to every person alive, I decided to focus on how it applies to people who fall outside of the norm, and who not only battle the existential dread intrinsic to life due to social interaction that everybody faces, but a different kind of dread and stress that results from being the "other" in a larger, social sense. For some, the lack of control over how one is perceived by others, or how one's self image is "distorted" by others, doesn't just result in discomfort.

This isn't all negative - the fact that two people can both have their image be distorted means that they can recognise each other and find safety in that. Those distorted individuals will find each other and create a force for good. My intention was that when looking at the painting, those who are themselves "distorted" can recognise themselves as belonging to the same group as the ones in the painting, while those who subject others to distortion are made uncomfortable and unwelcome. It's meant to be a call for recognition, a plea for all of us to come together, and to fight against what keeps us subdued.

I applied slightly brown-tinted gesso to the canvas and left it for two days. I painted in the sky and clouds, and the next day I went over the clouds because I wasn't happy with how they looked. I painted in everything else, using the same dark purple for the road, hair and shadows. The last part was using thick layers of paint to represent the "distortion" of the figures faces. I thought I was done, but when it dried two days later I ended up painting over the faces again, adding more grey and warm (pink, orange) colours to differentiate it from the rest of the painting.

Ultimately, I think the painting is quite far from what I pictured. If I could do it again, I would have spent more time planning out details. In all the drafts I made, I only really put down the vague placements of things and their colours (which I ended up changing anyways), I didn't look much into how to actually paint them. For example, I wish I learnt more about clouds and what type I actually wanted to draw instead of completely relying on my mind. As with the "distortion" on the faces, I greatly underestimated how tough it is to make abstraction look intentional.

Like I said, it's a work in progress.
